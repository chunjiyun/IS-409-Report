1. Introduction

1.1 Composite Materials

Composite material is made from combining two or more constituent materials with distinct physical or chemical properties to create unique properties such as enhanced strength and reduced weight compared to the the original material alone. Generally, composite materials are the product of a fiber material and a matrix material. Fiber is the key to strength and stiffness and matrix material acts as a bonding agent, holding the fiber together and distributing loads across the composite. Common examples of composite materials include carbon fiber, glass fiber and aramid fiber.

1.2 Comparison Between Industry and In-House Composite Manufacturing Method

Today, composite materials are not only prevalent in large-scale industries but are also produced in low-volume production and customized applications, referred to as in-house manufacturing. There are significant differences between industrial and in-house manufacturing methods for producing composite parts. In industrial contexts, composite manufacturing often involves highly advanced technologies like automated fiber placement as seen in Figure 2. This technique employs robotic arms to precisely place narrow (0.3–3 cm) carbon fiber tapes onto a variety of surfaces, enabling the efficient creation of complex shapes and intricate corners with high precision.

On the other hand, in-house composite manufacturing is often done in lower production volumes, various part sizes and catered to the consumer needs. Instead of advanced machinery like automated fiber placement, in-house methods typically use a hand lay-up process on molds. Today, NUS FSAE uses in-house composite manufacturing to produce essential parts for their racing car. This includes larger structures like the front wing which is 1.3m x 0.5m x 0.06m (Figure 3) as well as an electronics box which can be as small as 15cm x 15cm x 15cm. My project scope is on the in-house manufacturing method.

1.3 Three Major Methods for In-House Carbon Fiber Lay-Up

In in-house manufacturing, there are three major methods used for carbon fiber lay-up. The first method is the wet lay-up process as seen in Figure 4. This is done by manually applying resin to the layers of dry fiber reinforcement. This requires handling wet resin and ensuring that the resin is evenly distributed with the right amount.

The second method is prepreg lay-up in Figure 5. Prepregs are carbon fiber sheets that are pre-impregnated with a partially cured resin system. Prepregs have a tacky texture and they can easily be cut to fit the mold it is the easiest to handle out of the three methods. Pre-pregs must be stored in refrigerated conditions to prevent full curing until the lay-up is completed.

The third and last method is resin transfer molding in Figure 6. It uses a resin injection system to inject the resin into a closed mold containing dry fibers.

While the three methods differ in the way resin is applied to the fiber, the procedures used to process these lay-ups are similar.



2. Pre-Preg Processing Method

This project uses prepreg due to its distinct advantages over other lay-up methods. Prepregs provide an excellent strength-to-weight ratio which is ideal for high performance applications where weight and mechanical properties are critical. For example, the front wing produced by NUS FSAE also uses prepregs for this reason. Additionally, prepregs offer consistent quality of composite parts because there is uniform fiber to resin ratio and consistent ply thickness. They are also easy to handle as it doesn’t require wet resins and this reduces the curing time since the resin system is already partially cured. 3. Challenges in Vacuum Bagging

Although the vacuum bagging process is essential for in-house composite manufacturing methods, it poses four major challenges related to skill dependency, time intensive, cost and environmental impact.

The first challenge is that vacuum bagging is a highly skill-dependent process. During the vacuuming phase, the vacuum bag can easily form folds on the lay-up especially for deep corners and complex shapes. These folds are very critical as they can lead to inconsistent vacuum pressure, resulting in bridging, voids, or pinholes that can compromise the strength and quality of the final composite part. Therefore, proper pleating process is required when vacuuming the bag to ensure that it’s fully conformed to the mold including intricate corners. This increases the complexity of the process and increases the demand for skilled labor, making it a challenging method for less experienced technicians to perform.

The second challenge is the time intensity of the process. Because there are several steps involved in the vacuum bagging process such as bagging, sealing and monitoring the conformity of the vacuum bag, this increases the overall production time. This reduces the productivity and efficiency of producing composite parts.

The third challenge is the high cost of vacuum bagging materials. At NUS, for instance, each roll of vacuum bagging film purchased every year costs $1562 SGD while the sealant tape costs $5.47 SGD per roll. Figure 10 shows the specifications of the materials purchased in NUS. Such expenses can add up quickly when the project scale increases hence reducing material costs could substantially lower production cost.

The last challenge is the significant waste produced in the process. The vacuum bag consists of several auxiliary materials as seen in Figure 8 and 9. All four materials are single-use materials leading to substantial waste. For example, the vacuum bagging film undergoes high plastic deformation hence cannot be reused. As all the materials needed for vacuum bagging are single-use, its environmental impact is great. Finding sustainable alternatives or reusable options for these materials would reduce waste and lessen the environmental footprint of composite manufacturing.

These challenges highlight the need for innovative solutions to make vacuum bagging less skill-dependent, more time and cost-efficient, and environmentally friendly. Addressing these challenges will contribute to a more sustainable approach to composite manufacturing.



4. Design Statement

Such challenges in the vacuum bagging process, leads to the design statement for this project:

Designing an integrated composite manufacturing system that reduces cost and environmental impact using a reusable vacuum bag while simplifying the process to reduce skill dependency, and accelerate the production time while ensuring high-quality composite parts. Hence, the project milestones are as follows:

1. Finding a reusable vacuum bagging material

2. Developing a composite prototype using the new vacuum bag material

3. Designing an integrated vacuum bagging system



5. Design Specifications for Reusable Vacuum Bagging Material

To establish the design specifications for reusable vacuum bagging material, the conventional vacuum bagging film, SL850 was used as the benchmark which can be seen in Figure 11.

Hence, the reusable vacuum bagging material should have the following design specifications:

1. Temperature: Withstand 180°C as that is the maximum curing temperature in the oven/autoclave

2. Elongation: Have at least 450% elongation rate for high conformity including deep corners

3. Reusability: The material can be reused and there is minimal plastic deformation after being reused. It is not contaminated by resin.

4. Accessibility: Easy to source in Singapore and low cost.

5. Ideally Translucent

Rubber’s maximum temperature tolerance is only 80°C which means it will melt at curing temperature. Also, rubber has high plastic deformation after stretching making it unsuitable for use.

In contrast, silicone can withstand temperatures up to 315°C and its elongation can reach up to 1490% depending on the thickness of the material.

Therefore, silicone was chosen as the reusable vacuum bagging material.

7. Cost Benefit Analysis of the Purchased Silicone

Defining the “reusability” in the design specification is done by comparing the cost per vacuum bag for both SL850 film and silicone sheet. SL850 vacuum bagging film has a length of 304m, width of 1.22m and a thickness of 0.0508mm and each roll costs S$1562. This means that for a part that uses 1m x 1m sized film, one roll can be used to do around 304 vacuum processes. Therefore, the reusability of the reusable vacuum bag is defined by whether the cost of one silicone sheet and reusing it can outweigh the cost of 304 SL850 vacuum bags. For SL850 vacuum bagging film, the cost per vacuum bag is:

1562 / 304 = S$5.14
For 0.2mm silicone sheet:
Length: 1m
Width: 0.5m
Thickness = 0.2mm
Price = S$11.31

S$11.31 / S$5.14 = 2.20

According to the calculation, reusing the silicone sheet 3 times outweighs the cost of making one vacuum bag using SL850. If the silicone sheet can be reused more than 3 times it is definitely cost-effective. This will be tested with an actual composite mold as part of future work.



8. ASTM D412 Experiment with Silicone

The significant limitation of the silicone purchased on AliExpress is that although it’s cheap, the elongation data is not provided. Therefore it is necessary to measure their elongation rate and analyze their mechanical behavior to assess their suitability for vacuum bagging applications. ASTM D412 was used as a guideline to conduct this assessment. ASTM D412 is a standard test method established by ASTM International for determining the tensile properties of rubber and elastomers which can be used for measuring key mechanical properties such as tensile strength, elongation at break, and stress-strain behavior under uniaxial tensile loading.

8.1. Experiment Objective

The objective of this experiment is to assess the suitability of silicone for vacuum bagging by measuring its elongation properties and identifying the appropriate material thickness.

8.2. Experiment Hypothesis

The hypothesis of the experiment is as follows:

The silicone’s elongation will meet the design specifications for reusable vacuum bagging material, while thinner specimens will elongate more than thicker specimens with less force required.

This hypothesis was established based on the known mechanical properties of silicone elastomers, which are characterized by high elasticity and the ability to undergo significant elongation without permanent deformation.

Furthermore, principles of material mechanics suggest that thinner specimens generally require less force to achieve the same level of elongation compared to thicker ones due to their reduced cross-sectional area and lower resistance to tensile forces.

8.3. Experiment Preparation

Before the experiment, there were several essential preparations for the experiment.

Creating a CAD drawing of the ASTM D412 Type C dogbone specimen:

SolidWorks software was used and this model accurately represented the standardized dimensions required for tensile testing, ensuring consistency with ASTM specifications. Figure 14 shows the CAD drawing.

Fabrication of acrylic template:

The CAD drawing was imported into a laser cutting machine to produce an acrylic template of the dogbone specimen. Laser cutting provided high precision, resulting in an exact replica of the CAD design. Figure 15 shows the laser-cut templates.

Making silicone specimens:

The acrylic template was placed onto silicone sheets of varying thicknesses. Using a scalpel knife, the outline of the template was carefully traced and cut from the silicone material. See Figure 16. A total of 16 silicone specimens were prepared, where there were four specimens for each thickness category being tested.

8.4. Experiment Procedure

An Instron 34TM-5 universal testing machine was used to conduct the tensile tests on the silicone specimens. The machine was configured with a grip-to-grip separation of 33 mm and a gauge length of 25 mm to accommodate the standardized specimen dimensions. Grip pressure was maintained at 100 psi (689 kPa) to ensure secure holding of the specimens without causing premature failure or slippage during testing.

The tests were performed at the speed of 500 ± 50 mm/min, adhering to the guidelines specified in ASTM D412. Each silicone specimen was elongated until breakage occurred to determine the material's ultimate elongation and tensile strength. 8.5. Experiment Results and Analysis

During the experiment, the testing machine collected multiple displacement readings for the same force value, resulting in a one-to-many relationship between force and displacement data points. For example, at a force of 0.001 kN, several displacement measurements were recorded. When plotted, this data produced a graph (see Figure X) that made it difficult to determine the elongation at specific force levels.

To resolve this issue, data cleaning was performed to establish a one-to-one mapping between force and displacement. The following steps were taken:

1. Identify the Failure Point: The point at which each specimen failed was identified in the dataset.

2. Sort Force Data: Force values were sorted in ascending order from 0 N up to the failure point for each specimen.

3. Generate New Force Dataset: A new set of force values was created, starting from 0 N with increments of 0.0001 kN.

4. Average Displacement Values: For each force value in the new dataset, the average displacement was calculated from the multiple displacement readings corresponding to that force. This ensured a one-to-one correspondence between force and displacement.

5. Use Raw Data Beyond Failure Point: From the failure point onwards, the raw force and displacement data were retained without modification.

6. Create Scatter Plot: A scatter plot was generated using the cleaned force and displacement data, resulting in a clearer representation of the material's behavior (see Figure X).

This data cleaning process was repeated for specimens with thicknesses of 0.2 mm, 0.5 mm, 1 mm, and 2 mm to ensure consistency and accuracy across all tests.

Figure 18 and 19 shows the compiled scatter plot The experiment showed that 0.5mm thickness elongates the most with the least force required to stretch to that point. This can be seen by the orange line that stretches the furthest in Figure 18. 2mm (purple) and 1.5mm both broke at similar elongation. However, 2mm needed (green) needed the greatest force to elongate.

Figure 18 shows a clear trend where thicker specimens require more force to reach similar displacement values. The 2.0 mm specimen, represented in purple, shows the highest force values for a given displacement, followed by the 1.0 mm, 0.5 mm, and 0.2 mm specimens. Thinner specimens (0.2 mm) reach higher displacements at much lower forces, indicating greater flexibility but lower strength compared to thicker specimens.

According to Figure 19, 0.5 mm specimen has the highest elongation at break (301 mm) and percentage elongation (1200%), indicating it can stretch the most before breaking. This suggests an optimal balance of flexibility and strength for this thickness. The 0.2 mm specimen shows significant elongation (167 mm, 668%), but due to its low force threshold (0.0066 kN), it is less resistant to higher forces, making it weaker than the thicker specimens. The 1.0 mm and 2.0 mm specimens exhibit reduced elongation percentages (760% and 716%, respectively) but can withstand higher forces (0.0324 kN and 0.06055 kN, respectively), indicating greater strength than 0.2mm.

Three 0.2mm specimens slipped off from the machine grip hence they are not included in the graph.
